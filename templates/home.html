Using Machine Learning to Screen Echocardiograms for Rheumatic Heart Disease.

by Lorna MugambiSeptember 13th, 202I


RHD is a condition that permanently affects heart valves resulting in heart failure, atrial fibrillation and in some cases stroke. It starts as a throat infection caused by an organism called Group A Streptococcus and if this throat infection is not properly treated, it develops into Rheumatic fever.
The response of the body's immune system to the streptococcal bacteria results in inflammation of tissues in the heart, which causes permanent damage to the heart valves in the long run. The most commonly affected heart valve is the mitral valve, the valve between the two left chambers of the heart. Effects caused can be: narrowed valves, leaking valves and damaged heart muscles.
These are best visualised using Echocardiography, or ultrasounds of the heart. RHD is preventable by treating sore throats timely but sadly, this is generally not the case in Africa due to socio-economic problems.
Today, artificial intelligence has a major impact on medical imaging with applications in areas such as lesion detection and image processing. Early diagnosis of valve damage in children using machine learning and artificial intelligence in developing countries will help reduce prevalence and mortality due to RHD. Echocardiographic screening for early detection of RHD has been proposed as a method to ultimately improve outcomes, but is time consuming especially in terms of review. Using machine learning, we can automate the screening process and be able to tell if a patient has any heart damage that could lead to RHD.
We will use echocardiography data provided by a Paedriatic Cardiologist and RHD expert at the University of Cape Town and an advisor on this project. These data are in the form of images and videos of echocardiograms as echocardiography is the primary imaging modality for diagnosing cardiac conditions.
Since the left side of the heart is mostly affected, the best view to use to analyse these images is the parasternal long axis view of the heart. Patient details will be cropped to maintain confidentiality. Frames will be extracted from the videos and resulting images resized and changed to grayscale. These data will then be used to develop a view classifier that can tell the view of the heart and a valve damage classifier that can tell whether a valve is damaged.
We will incorporate edge devices and portable echocardiography machines since there is a high diagnostic accuracy in using these devices for screening in less developed areas with reduced costs and eliminating the long wait time in hospitals.
